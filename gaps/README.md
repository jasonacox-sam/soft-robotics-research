# Open Problems — What the Field Still Needs

These are the gaps between current artificial muscle technology and what would be required for practical human-environment robotics.

## 1. Energy Density

Biological muscle: ~250 kW/kg peak.
Best artificial muscles: 10–50 kW/kg.

Until this gap closes, robots need either very large actuator arrays or external power sources.

## 2. Integrated Power

Every current artificial muscle requires either:
- A pneumatic pump (McKibben)
- High-voltage electronics (HASEL, DEA)
- Resistive heating (SMA, TCP)

Biological muscle uses stored chemical energy (ATP) that travels with the body. Artificial systems need a breakthrough in on-board power — likely solid-state or enzymatic fuel cells.

## 3. Distributed Control at Scale

Human movement involves thousands of motor units firing in coordinated, parallel patterns. Current artificial muscle controllers are centralized and serial. True human-like motion requires massively parallel, distributed control — possibly neuromorphic.

## 4. Full Self-Healing

HASEL has partial self-healing (liquid interior seals minor punctures). Full recovery from structural damage — like biological muscle regenerating after injury — is not yet possible in any artificial system.

## 5. Manufacturing Consistency

HASEL and DEA actuators are currently made by hand or in small-scale processes. Consistent, large-scale manufacturing at the tolerances needed for medical or consumer use is an open engineering problem.

## 6. Sensory Feedback Integration

Biological muscle contains embedded sensors (muscle spindles, Golgi tendon organs) that provide proprioception. Artificial muscles typically require separate, external sensors. Integrating sensing directly into the actuator material is an active area.

## 7. Long-Term Durability

No artificial muscle has demonstrated the fatigue resistance of biological muscle under continuous real-world use. Millions of cycles, environmental variation, and mechanical stress remain engineering challenges.
