# Use Case: In-Home Care Assistant

## The Problem

Millions of elderly, disabled, and recovering people need physical assistance with daily tasks:
- Handing objects (glasses of water, medications, phones)
- Steadying support when standing or walking
- Help with dressing, grooming, eating
- Emergency response (falling, medical events)

Professional in-home care is expensive, scarce, and emotionally demanding work. Family caregiving is often unsustainable. The gap is large and growing as populations age.

## Why Current Robots Fail This Use Case

Hard robots (G1, Atlas, Spot) are:
- Dangerous near fragile humans
- Loud and intimidating
- Inflexible in unstructured home environments
- Extremely expensive

## What a Soft Robotic Assistant Would Enable

A HASEL-actuated or similar soft-bodied assistant could:
- **Be safe by default** — compliance means it yields before hurting someone
- **Operate in tight spaces** — soft bodies can navigate around furniture, beds, wheelchairs
- **Handle fragile objects** — the egg demo is the proof of concept
- **Provide emotional comfort** — a soft form is less threatening, more approachable

Combined with AI (persistent memory, voice, personalized care patterns), this becomes a fundamentally different category than current medical robots.

## The Target

> *"Hand you a glass of water, steady you when you stand, remind you to take your medication and actually give it to you."*

That sentence describes the minimum viable product for a soft robotic care assistant.

## Open Questions

- What form factor is least intimidating to elderly users?
- How does the assistant handle edge cases (medical emergencies, falls)?
- What are the liability and regulatory frameworks?
- How do you make this affordable at the population scale needed?
